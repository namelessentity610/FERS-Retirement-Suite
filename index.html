Your pay inputs

Additional creditable pay covers things like Law Enforcement Availability Pay (LEAP), Administratively Uncontrollable Overtime (AUO) pay under 5 U.S.C. 5545(c)(2), or a recruitment/retention incentive that is structured as part of basic pay under 5 CFR 550.103. Shift, night, Sunday, and holiday differential pay for most GS employees is premium pay, not basic pay — like overtime, awards, and bonuses, it generally does not count toward your High-3, even though it shows up in your paycheck. AUO is the main exception: where it applies (mainly certain criminal investigators / law-enforcement positions), AUO premium pay is treated as basic pay for retirement. If you're not sure whether a differential you receive counts, leave this at $0 and check your SF-50 / LES, or your pay code, with HR.

How High-3 & the annuity work
The 2,087-hour divisor & what counts as basic pay

For most federal employees, the hourly rate of basic pay is the annual GS rate (including locality) divided by 2,087 — the standard number of hours in a work year. Your Creditable Basic Pay for High-3 purposes is essentially your annual GS rate (locality-adjusted) plus any pay that is itself defined as "basic pay" under 5 CFR 550.103 — most commonly LEAP for criminal investigators, or certain recruitment/retention incentives if structured as basic pay.

What does NOT count: overtime, awards and bonuses, most allowances, danger/hazard pay, and non-foreign area cost-of-living allowances (COLA). These can inflate your gross pay without raising your High-3.
Shift & differential pay — the most common point of confusion: night, Sunday, holiday, and other shift differentials are premium pay under 5 U.S.C. 5545(a)/(b), not basic pay — they're excluded from High-3 for the great majority of GS employees, the same way they're excluded for firefighters. The one significant exception is Administratively Uncontrollable Overtime (AUO) pay, 5 U.S.C. 5545(c)(2) — paid to some criminal investigators and similar positions in lieu of regular overtime — which is creditable basic pay. If you receive a recurring differential and aren't sure which category it falls into, your pay code on your LES (and your SF-50 remarks) will say; HR can confirm in a minute.
Why the highest 3 consecutive years matter

High-3 is the average of your three highest consecutive years of Creditable Basic Pay. A promotion or a within-grade/locality increase during that window raises your High-3 — and therefore your lifetime annuity. Use the optional rows above if you want to compare a window that includes an upcoming promotion.

TSP essentials
FERS matching — capture the full agency contribution

FERS employees get: 1% automatic agency contribution (regardless of what you put in), plus dollar-for-dollar match on your first 3% of Creditable Basic Pay, plus 50 cents on the dollar for the next 2%. Full match requires you contribute at least 5% — the agency puts in 5% and you put in 5%, for 10% total into your TSP from the first dollar of your career.

The match is computed against Creditable Basic Pay, not gross pay — so the figure from the Pay tab is the right one to use.

The five core funds, plus the L Funds

TSP has five individual funds, each with a different risk/return profile:

G FundGovernment securities. No risk of loss of principal; returns track short-term Treasury rates (~4–5%). The only fund guaranteed not to lose value, but barely outpaces inflation long-term.
F FundBond index (U.S. investment-grade bonds). Moderate, steady returns; moves opposite to interest rates.
C FundS&P 500 index — large U.S. companies. ~10%/yr long-run average, but with significant year-to-year swings.
S FundSmall/mid-cap U.S. stocks (everything outside the S&P 500). Higher growth potential, higher volatility than C.
I FundInternational developed-market stocks. Adds geographic diversification; returns vary with currency and foreign markets.

The L Funds (Lifecycle) are pre-mixed combinations of all five, automatically rebalanced and gradually shifted toward G/F as the target date approaches. L 2035 or L 2040 are reasonable defaults if you plan to retire around that year — the fund handles the rebalancing automatically. L Income is the most conservative option, designed for retirees already drawing down their balance.

Most planners suggest moving toward income-oriented allocations (L Income, or a heavier G/F mix) in the 5–10 years before retirement to reduce the chance of a market downturn hitting right before you need the money.

Roth TSP vs Traditional TSP

Traditional TSP: contributions reduce taxable income now; withdrawals — both contributions and growth — are taxed as ordinary income in retirement. Better if you expect a lower tax rate in retirement.

Roth TSP: contributions are after-tax; qualified withdrawals (both contributions and growth) are tax-free. Better if you expect a higher rate later, or want tax-free income in retirement to reduce AGI and thus your Medicare premiums (IRMAA).

The 59½ catch: a Roth withdrawal is tax-free on the growth portion only if it is "qualified" — meaning both (a) 5 years have passed since your first Roth TSP contribution, and (b) you are age 59½ or older (or disabled). If you withdraw before 59½, the growth portion of a Roth withdrawal is taxed as ordinary income — even though your original contributions come out tax-free either way. TSP withdrawals are pro-rata, so you cannot choose to withdraw "just contributions" the way you sometimes can with a Roth IRA.

Given the substantial other income (annuity + SRS, if eligible + household income) you may have in early retirement, Roth contributions during high-earning working years can still be valuable — but run the numbers with a tax professional.

Withdrawing TSP early — the age-55 separation rule

Normally, TSP withdrawals before age 59½ trigger a 10% early withdrawal penalty on top of regular income tax. Regular FERS employees get a narrower exception than special-category (firefighter/LEO/ATC) employees: if you separate from federal service in or after the calendar year you turn 55, withdrawals from TSP made after that separation are exempt from the 10% penalty — even though you are under 59½.

What this exception does not change: it removes the 10% penalty only. It does not make a Roth withdrawal "qualified." So:
  • Traditional TSP: withdraw penalty-free starting at separation (age 55+ in the year of separation); pay ordinary income tax as usual.
  • Roth TSP: withdraw penalty-free, but if you are under 59½ and have not met the 5-year rule, the growth portion of each withdrawal is still taxed as ordinary income (pro-rata with your contributions). Once you turn 59½ and the 5-year clock has run, Roth withdrawals become fully tax-free.

If you retire under MRA+10 before age 55 (e.g., at your MRA of 56 or 57 — generally not possible below 55 since MRA itself is 55-57), this exception still applies since MRA ≥ 55 for everyone. If you separate before the year you turn 55 for any other reason, the 10% penalty applies to withdrawals until 59½ unless another exception (like substantially equal periodic payments) applies. Confirm your situation with a tax professional before separating.

SS claiming age comparison
Key insight: Claiming at 62 means earlier payments but a permanently reduced benefit. Waiting to 67 or 70 means a larger check for life. The breakeven is typically around age 78–82.
How the 62/67/70 figures are calculated: SS takes your highest 35 years of inflation-adjusted earnings, averages them (your "AIME"), and runs that average through a formula (your "PIA") to get your age-67 benefit. Age 62 = PIA reduced for claiming early; age 70 = PIA increased for delaying. If you stop working before age 62 with fewer than 35 years of earnings, the missing years count as $0 in the average — pulling all three figures down. ssa.gov's projection assumes you keep earning until each claiming age, so it can overstate your real benefit if you retire early.

SRS rules in plain language
Who actually gets the SRS

The SRS is paid only to employees who retire on an immediate, unreduced annuity before age 62 — that means the MRA+30 path or the 60-with-20 path. It is not paid to:

  • employees retiring under MRA+10 (the reduced, "postponed-or-not" early retirement), even though they are also retiring before 62; and
  • employees retiring under 62-with-5, since they are already 62 and Social Security is available immediately.

Your eligibility path is determined on the High-3 & Annuity tab.

How the SRS amount is calculated

Formula: SRS = (age-62 SS estimate) × (years of FERS service ÷ 40), paid monthly from your retirement date until the month you turn 62.

Example: $1,800/mo SS estimate × (25 ÷ 40) = $1,125/mo SRS. Because your federal years are typically less than 40, the SRS is a fraction of your full SS benefit.

The age-62 figure from ssa.gov overstates your actual benefit as an early retiree because the projection assumes continued earnings. The SRS itself is not affected by this — it uses the ssa.gov figure as-is.

The earnings test — when it applies and what it costs

For regular FERS retirees who receive the SRS (MRA+30 or 60+20 — both already at or above MRA), the Social Security earnings test applies from the start of SRS payments. Each year, earned income above the annual exempt amount (~$23K, indexed) reduces the SRS by about $1 for every $2 over the limit.

Your spouse's income does not count. Only your own earned income (wages, self-employment net) counts.

Practical implication: If you retire at your MRA (say 57) with 30 years and take a part-time job earning $35K/yr, the test applies immediately and would reduce the SRS by roughly $6K/yr — but you were also earning $35K, so the net picture is usually still positive.
Coordinating SRS with actual Social Security

The SRS stops the month you turn 62 — you then decide when to claim actual Social Security (62, FRA ~67, or 70). These are two completely separate decisions:

  • The SRS amount is fixed by the formula and cannot be increased by waiting.
  • Actual SS grows ~8%/year for each year you delay from 62 to 70.
  • You can take the SRS in full and still wait to claim SS at 70 — the two do not interact.

If you are in good health and have other income during ages 62 through 70, waiting to claim SS at 70 is often optimal, as it maximizes the inflation-protected lifetime annuity.

The four retirement paths (5 U.S.C. 8412)
62 + 5Age 62 with at least 5 years of service. Immediate, unreduced. Uses the 1.1% multiplier for your entire annuity if you have 20+ years; otherwise 1.0%.
60 + 20Age 60 with at least 20 years. Immediate, unreduced, 1.0% multiplier. SRS-eligible.
MRA + 30Your MRA with at least 30 years. Immediate, unreduced, 1.0% multiplier. SRS-eligible.
MRA + 10Your MRA with at least 10 (but fewer than 30) years. Immediate, but reduced 5% for every year (5/12% per month) you are under age 62 — unless you postpone the annuity until later to reduce or eliminate the cut. Not SRS-eligible.

There is also deferred retirement (leave your contributions in, claim later, no SRS, annuity based on High-3 and service at the time you left) — not modeled in this calculator.

Annuity computation (5 U.S.C. 8415)
Standard: 1.0% × High-3 × years of service
Enhanced: 1.1% × High-3 × years of service — only if you retire at age 62 or later with at least 20 years of service (applies to the whole calculation, not just years over 20)

Example: $110,000 High-3, 25 years, retiring at 62 → 1.1% × 25 × $110,000 = $30,250/yr. The same person retiring at 60 (1.0%) would get $27,500/yr — a meaningful reason to consider working to 62 if you're close.

Unused sick leave converts to additional service time and is added to your years of service for the computation (not for meeting the eligibility thresholds above).

Survivor election & FEHB continuity

Electing a survivor annuity reduces your basic annuity: a 50% survivor benefit reduces it by 10%; a 25% survivor benefit reduces it by 5%.

Continued FEHB eligibility for a surviving spouse generally requires at least some survivor annuity to be in place. Treat the survivor election and health-coverage continuity as a single decision — you generally cannot add survivor benefits after retirement except in limited circumstances (e.g., marrying after retirement, with an election window).

COLAs (cost-of-living adjustments) generally do not apply to a regular FERS annuity before age 62, except for disability retirees and survivor annuitants. After 62, FERS COLAs are typically CPI minus 1 percentage point (capped), which is smaller than the full CPI adjustment CSRS retirees receive.

Pay mechanics — the High-3 foundation
Basic pay & the 2,087-hour year
  • Hourly rate (where relevant) = annual GS rate (with locality) ÷ 2,087
  • Creditable Basic Pay = annual GS rate (locality-adjusted) + any pay defined as "basic pay" under 5 CFR 550.103 (e.g., LEAP, certain incentive pay structured as basic pay)
  • Excluded: overtime, awards/bonuses, most differentials and allowances, non-foreign area COLA

If you're unsure whether a particular pay element counts, check your SF-50 remarks and your agency's payroll office — the safest default is to leave it out and let your official annuity estimate confirm or correct the figure.
